Background and Purpose

This brief describes what it means to become more data-driven, what is required to succeed, and which decisions the leadership team must make before work can begin.

The goal is not a large IT project. The goal is to give Brim Explorer access to insight about its own organisation, processes and customers — of sufficient quality to be actively used in governance and decision-making.

What Does It Mean to Be Data-Driven?

A data-driven organisation produces insight of sufficient quality for use in decision-making processes. It is not primarily about technology — it is about understanding your own business better.

In practice, this means three things:

  1. Decision support — managers and team leads have access to relevant insight when they need it, without having to request manual data exports.
  2. Automated consolidation — reports and overviews that are currently produced manually are generated automatically and consistently.
  3. Access to history — the ability to identify patterns over time, not just point-in-time snapshots.

It is important to have realistic expectations about sequencing. Advanced decision support does not come immediately. Early in the process, the work is about verifying that you have understood the data and that it reflects reality. Higher-level insight — with dependencies across systems — follows naturally once the foundation is in place.

Priority Insight Domains

We propose structuring the work around five domains that are critical to Brim Explorer's operations and growth:

Domain Examples of insight Priority
Finance Cost trends, liquidity overview, variance against budget, etc. High
Customers Passenger volume per route, seasonal trends, customer categories, etc. High
Employees Staffing overview, absence patterns, competency needs, turnover, etc. High
Vessel Construction Build status, milestones, cost vs. budget, supplier performance, etc. High
Operations & Maintenance Uptime, fault logs, maintenance history, energy consumption per vessel, etc. High

Finance and customers are proposed as the starting point, as they are closest to the core of business management and typically have data available in existing systems. Note: Operations & Maintenance may be equally accessible as a starting point — worth exploring.

What Is Required to Succeed

Technical competence can be sourced externally. What determines whether the project succeeds is largely internal enablement:

1. Leadership buy-in

The work must have clear backing from leadership — not just as approval, but as active understanding of the value proposition. Without it, the project stalls at the first prioritisation conflict.

2. Data access

We need access to the relevant systems: accounting system, booking data, HR system, vessel operations log, etc. Access must be clarified early, as this is often where projects encounter unexpected obstacles.

3. Business-side resources

Insight without business understanding is worthless. We need people internally who:

  • Know which insight is relevant and should be prioritised
  • Can help verify that what is built actually reflects reality
  • Are willing to use the solution actively throughout the process

4. Internal champions

The most successful data projects are driven by concrete needs from concrete people. We need 2–3 internal champions with specific problems they want to solve — and who are willing to test and provide feedback on an ongoing basis.

5. Specialists when needed

Once the solution is mature enough, it may be relevant to bring in specialists in AI/ML, market analysis or similar disciplines. This is step two, not step one.

Proposed Approach and Steps

We recommend a phased approach rather than a large project with a long time horizon:

  1. Discovery (2–4 weeks) — Identify which data exists, where it lives, and what quality challenges are present. Interview 3–5 internal stakeholders about their most important decision-making needs.
  2. Pilot on one domain (4–8 weeks) — Build a simple, working insight solution for one priority domain (recommended: Finance or Customers). Verify that data and logic align with reality.
  3. Expansion (ongoing) — After a successful pilot: gradually extend to the remaining domains based on prioritised needs and available capacity.

This approach minimises risk, delivers visible value quickly, and builds internal trust in the solution.

What This Is Not

  • A data warehouse project that takes 18 months before anyone sees anything.
  • An AI project. AI may come in at a later stage, but is not a prerequisite.
  • An IT project driven by the technology department without business ownership.

Recommended Next Steps

We ask the leadership team to take a position on the following:

  1. Principle approval — Does the leadership team agree that this is the right priority now?
  2. Resource clarification — Who internally will own the project and serve as champion?
  3. Technical clarification — The CTO is given a mandate to define the technology and security framework by [date].
  4. Kick-off meeting — Set a date for the first working session with relevant internal resources.
